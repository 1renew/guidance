Guidance

S011C: Supplementary winter bird feeding

Increase bird populations by spreading seed for them during the winter and spring when their natural food is harder to find.

Published 17 June 2020
From: Natural England

Contents

Why farmland birds need extra food in winter

Farmland bird species have declined since the 1970s. This is partly due to changing farming menthods which has decreased the availability of seeds in winter and early spring.

Spreading seed on your land is a simple and effective way to increase the availability of food for farmland birds.

Seed-eating farmland birds include finches, buntings and partridges. Bird ID guides are available:

  • Game and Wildlife Conservation Trust's (GWCT) Big Farmland Bird Count
  • Royal Society for the Protection of Birds (RSPB) Bird Identifier

When to start spreading seed.

Start spreading seed when other seed sources on your farm start to become depleted. The time this happens varies and depends on:

Supplementary feed alongside:

  • the amount of natural and sown seed available in the autumn
  • the number of birds
  • how harsh the weather is

As a guide, feed from December through to April.

You should start feeding from the beginning of November if your winter bird food plots have failed or it is the first winter of an autumn-sown winter bird food plot. This will replace your winter bird food plots as a source.

Choosing appropriate feeding sites

You need to spread seed at more than one site to increase availability and provide seed for species which prefer to feed in different locations. For example, tree sparrows and yellowhammers like to have nearby cover, such as a hedge. Corn buntings prefer sites next to fences or ditches and with vantage points to perch and scan for predators.

Top up the seed at each site on different days to make sure there is always some seed available. You will also reduce the risk of attracting pests, such as rats.

Spread seed on firm, free-draining areas of bare ground or short vegetation to make it easy for birds to find. If you cannot easily see the seeds then the vegetation is too long.

Suitable areas include:

  • winter food bird plots
  • overwinter stubble
  • cultivated fallow areas
  • bare or sparsely vegetated farm tracks in quiet areas
  • areas of hard standing away from farmyards, such as a beet pad
  • regularly mown grass margins with short vegetation

You can alter your seed mix to meet the specific needs of certain species. You can adjust your seed mix if you know one of these species is present in your area:

  • corn bunting - feed mostly wheat with smaller amounts of naked oats and barley
  • tree sparrow - increase the amount of red millet and sunflower hearts that you feed
  • grey partridge - increase the proportion of cereals that you feed

When to feed

The best time to start feeding is when other seed sources of your farm are starting to become depleted. This will vary and depends on the amount of natural and sown seed available in the autumn, the number of birds and how harsh the weather is. As a guide, start feeding from December through to April.

You should start feeding from the beginning of November if your winter bird food plots have failed or if it is the first winter of an autumn-sown plot. This will replace your plots as a food source.

How much to feed

Your aim is to always have food available at your feeding sites. You should not leave a site for more than a day without food.

To start with, feed around 25kg of seed per week at each feeding site. Adjust the amount of seed to match that needed by the birds. Each feeding situation will be different, so you should check feeding sites regularly and replace seed when most of it has been eaten.

Increase the frequency or the volume of feeding if needed. Birds need more seed during periods of cold weather, particular if other food sources are unavailable due to frost or snow.

There should not be large amounts of seed left at the end of each week. If seed is left uneaten, lower the amount of seed to reduce waste and consumption by deer, rats and pigeons.

If you have more than one feeding site, replace the seed at each site on a different day. This will make sure there is always seed available on your land.

How to spread seed

Broadcast seed either by hand or using equipment, such as a well-cleaned slug pellet applicator or purpose-built spreader.

Aim for an even spread of seed. It is important not to spread seed too thickly, as this may encourage diseases and parasites harmful to farmland birds, as well as pests such as rats.

To maintain good hygiene and reduce the risks of disease transmission between birds at your supplementary feeding sites:

  • spin or scatter seed only, to avoid creating piles of seed or long trails
  • check the seed consumption and only replace seed when most of it is eaten
  • move feeding sites every year, to reduce pathogen build-up
  • you can move feeding sites during the same winter, but no more than 50m at a time
  • stop feeding for at least a week if there is a visible build-up of uneaten food
